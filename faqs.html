Under FEMA rules, if an Indian resident receives foreign currency from the sale of overseas securities (including ESOPs/RSUs), the proceeds should generally be repatriated to India or reinvested in permissible overseas assets within 180 days. You should consult a qualified CA or legal advisor regarding your specific situation and applicable timelines.
ESOP and RSU taxation in India is complex and has two components: (1) at vesting/exercise — the spread (fair market value minus exercise price) is taxed as perquisite income under your salary; (2) on sale — any further gain is taxed as capital gain (LTCG or STCG depending on the holding period). Platizio recommends consulting a qualified tax advisor for ESOP/RSU-specific guidance.
Note: Tax laws change frequently. The information below reflects general rules as understood at the time of publication and should not be relied upon as tax advice. Always consult a qualified Chartered Accountant for guidance specific to your situation.
US stocks are treated as foreign assets for Indian tax purposes. Gains from shares held for more than 24 months qualify as Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) and are taxed at 12.5% (without indexation benefit). Gains from shares held for 24 months or less are Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG) and are taxed at your applicable income tax slab rate.
Dividends paid by US companies are subject to a 25% US withholding tax at source under the India-US DTAA (reduced from the standard 30%). The net dividend (after US withholding) is then included in your total income in India and taxed at your applicable slab rate. You can claim a foreign tax credit (Form 67) for the US tax already withheld, to avoid double taxation.
DTAA stands for Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement. India and the USA have a DTAA that prevents the same income from being taxed in full in both countries. For dividends, it reduces the US withholding tax to 25% for Indian residents. For capital gains, the DTAA generally assigns taxing rights to India only — meaning US capital gains tax does not apply to Indian resident investors.
For filing your Indian income tax return, you will typically need:
  • Schedule FA — to declare foreign assets (your US brokerage account)
  • Schedule FSI — to report foreign-sourced income (dividends, capital gains)
  • Schedule TR — to claim foreign tax relief
  • Form 67 — to claim credit for US taxes withheld on dividends
Your account statement and P&L report from Platizio, along with Form 1042-S (issued by the US broker for dividend withholding), will help you complete these schedules.
For Indian income tax purposes, the relevant period is the Indian financial year (April 1 to March 31). However, for Schedule FA (foreign asset disclosure), the reporting period is the calendar year (January 1 to December 31) of the relevant accounting period. Your CA can help you reconcile these dates.
No. Under the India-US DTAA, capital gains on the sale of US-listed shares by Indian tax residents are taxable only in India, not in the US. You do not need to file a US tax return solely on account of capital gains from US stocks as an Indian resident investor.
There is no separate "currency gain" tax in India for individual investors holding foreign stocks. Your capital gain (or loss) is calculated in INR using the exchange rate at the time of purchase and at the time of sale — so currency movements are implicitly included in your capital gain calculation.
